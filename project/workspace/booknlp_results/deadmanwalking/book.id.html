In 1982, Sister Helen Prejean begins corresponding with a death row inmate at Louisiana State Penitentiary in Angola, Elmo Patrick Sonnier. Prejean has recently gone through a spiritual transformation and renewed her (Prejean) commitment to a life of social justice. During a drive to Angola, he (Millard) describes the history of capital punishment and the legal and political decisions that have shaped it. Millard paints a portrait of an arbitrary system that determines who lives and who dies primarily on the basis of race and class. LeBlanc reprimands Prejean for not speaking to the victims' families as well as to the killers. The Pardon Board votes in favor of Patrick's execution.
