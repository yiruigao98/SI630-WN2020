Animal Dreams opens with a brief chapter narrated in the third person, from the point of view of Doc Homer. This establishes a double narrative voice, which switches between dreams and memories of the past and events of the present. Doc Homer remembers his (Homer) daughters, Codi and Hallie, when they were young. Their mother is dead. In the second chapter, narrated by Codi in first person, the plot line begins. Hallie leaves Tucson, Arizona, where she (Hallie) was living with Codi and Carlo, for Nicaragua. She (Hallie) plans to assist the newly established communist regime with their crop cultivation. Shortly thereafter, Codi also leaves Tucson, returning to her (Hallie) small rural hometown, Grace, to care for her (Hallie) ailing father and to teach high school biology. The return to Grace is fraught with difficulty for Codi, as she (Grace) has always felt herself an outsider in the town and has never had a very close relationship with her (Grace) father. Her (Grace) return home raises the specter of several mysteries surrounding Codi and her (Grace) family's past: her (Grace) failure to hold a medical license despite her (Grace) attendance at medical school, the deaths of her (Grace) mother and of her (Grace) child, and the relationship of her (Grace) family to the rest of the community. In Grace, Codi stays in her (Grace) friend Emelina Domingos's guest house. As the two women talk, Codi's high school relationship with Loyd Peregrina is revealed. Loyd, a friend of Emelina's husband J.T., still lives and works in town. Re-visiting Grace, Codi is again struck by her (Grace) feeling of being an outsider. Codi and Hallie's mother died shortly after Hallie's birth. At the age of fifteen, Codi became pregnant with and then miscarried Loyd's child. She (Hallie) never told anyone. Her (Hallie) father, the town doctor, was aware of the situation, but Codi still does not know this. Codi and Loyd meet again and begin a new relationship. Loyd, a Native American who grew up on the nearby Reservation, is ready to establish a serious and committed relationship, but Codi is not ready to imagine herself as staying in one place or loving only one person. Loyd accepts her (Hallie) ambivalence. They continue to see each other, and he (Loyd) teaches her (Hallie) about Native American Cultures. Meanwhile, the town of Grace faces a terrible threat to its very existence. The local Black Mountain Mine has been dumping sulfuric acid and other chemicals into the water supply for years. This water is necessary for the irrigation of the pecan and fruit trees that are integral to the community's survival. While the men of town notified the Environmental Protection Agency of the problem, the only solution the EPA requires, that the river be dammed above the town, is almost worse than the problem.
