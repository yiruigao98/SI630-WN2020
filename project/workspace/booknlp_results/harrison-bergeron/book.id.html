Text

It is the year 2081. Because of Amendments 211, 212, and 213 to the Constitution, every American is fully equal, meaning that no one is stupider, uglier, weaker, or slower than anyone else. The Handicapper General and a team of agents ensure that the laws of equality are enforced. One April, fourteen-year-old Harrison Bergeron is taken away from his (Bergeron) parents, George and Hazel, by the government. George and Hazel aren't fully aware of the tragedy. Hazel's lack of awareness is due to average intelligence. In 2081, those who possess average intelligence are unable to think for extended stretches of time. George can't comprehend the tragedy because the law requires him (George) to wear a radio twenty-four hours a day. The government broadcasts noise over these radios to interrupt the thoughts of intelligent people like George. Hazel and George are watching ballerinas dance on TV. Hazel has been crying, but she (Hazel) can't remember why. She (Hazel) remarks on the prettiness of the dance. For a few moments, George reflects on the dancers, who are weighed down to counteract their gracefulness and masked to counteract their good looks. They have been handicapped so that TV viewers won't feel bad about their own appearance. Because of their handicaps, the dancers aren't very good. A noise interrupts George's thoughts. Two of the dancers onscreen hear the noise, too; apparently, they are smart and must wear radios as well. Hazel says she (Hazel) would enjoy hearing the noises that the handicappers dream up. George seems skeptical. If she (Hazel) were Handicapper General, Hazel says, she (Hazel) would create a chime noise to use on Sundays, which she (Hazel) thinks would produce a religious effect. The narrator explains that Hazel strongly resembles Diana Moon Glampers, Handicapper General. Hazel says she (Hazel) would be a good Handicapper General, because she (Hazel) knows what normalcy is. Before being interrupted by another noise, George thinks of his (George) son, Harrison. Hazel thinks George looks exhausted and urges him (George) to lie down and rest his (George)

``handicap bag,'' (him)
forty-seven pounds of weight placed in a bag and locked around George's neck. He (George) says he (George) hardly notices the weight anymore. Hazel suggests taking a few of the weights out of the bag, but he (George) says if everyone broke the law, society would return to its old competitive ways. Hazel says she (Hazel) would hate that. A noise interrupts the conversation, and George can't remember what they were talking about. On TV, an announcer with a speech impediment attempts to read a bulletin. He (George) can't overcome his (George) impediment, so he (George) hands the bulletin to a ballerina to read. Hazel commends him (George) for working with his (George) God-given abilities and says he (George) should get a raise simply for trying so hard. The ballerina begins reading in her (ballerina) natural, beautiful voice, then apologizes and switches to a growly voice that won't make anyone jealous. The bulletin says that Harrison has escaped from prison. A photo of Harrison appears on the screen. He (Harrison) is wearing the handicaps meant to counteract his (Harrison) strength, intelligence, and good looks. The photo shows that he (Harrison) is seven feet tall and covered in 300 pounds of metal. He (Harrison) is wearing huge earphones, rather than a small radio, and big glasses meant to blind him (Harrison) and give him (Harrison) headaches. He (Harrison) is also wearing a red rubber nose and black caps over his (Harrison) teeth. His (Harrison) eyebrows are shaved off. After a rumbling noise, the photo on the Bergerons' TV screen is replaced with an image of Harrison himself, who has stormed the studio. He (Harrison) says that he (Harrison) is the emperor, the greatest ruler in history, and that everyone must obey him (Harrison). Then he (Harrison) rips off all of his (Harrison) handicaps. He (Harrison) looks like a god. He (Harrison) says that the first woman brave enough to stand up will be his (Harrison) empress. A ballerina rises to her (ballerina) feet. Harrison removes her (ballerina) handicaps and mask, revealing a beautiful woman. He (Harrison) orders the musicians to play, saying he (Harrison) will make them royalty if they do their best. Unhappy with their initial attempt, Harrison conducts, waving a couple of musicians in the air like batons, and sings. They try again and do better. After listening to the music, Harrison and his (Harrison) empress dance. Defying gravity, they move through the air, flying thirty feet upward to the ceiling, which they kiss. Then, still in the air, they kiss each other. Diana Moon Glampers comes into the studio and kills Harrison and the empress with a shotgun. Training the gun on the musicians, she (empress) orders them to put their handicaps on. The Bergerons' screen goes dark. George, who has left the room to get a beer, returns and asks Hazel why she (Hazel) has been crying. She (Hazel) says something sad happened on TV, but she (Hazel) can't remember exactly what. He (George) urges her (Hazel) not to remember sad things. A noise sounds in George's head, and Hazel says it sounded like a doozy. He (George) says she (Hazel) can say that again, and she (Hazel) repeats that it sounded like a doozy.
