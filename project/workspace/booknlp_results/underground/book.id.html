The anonymous narrator of Notes from Underground is a bitter, misanthropic man living alone in St. Petersburg, Russia, in the 1860s. He (man) is a veteran of the Russian civil service who has recently been able to retire because he (man) has inherited some money. The novel consists of the

``notes'' (the man)
that the man writes, a confused and often contradictory set of memoirs or confessions describing and explaining his (man) alienation from modern society. Notes from Underground is divided into two sections. The first,
``Underground,'' (the Man)
is shorter and set in the 1860s, when the Underground Man is forty years old. This section serves as an introduction to the character of the Underground Man, explaining his (Man) theories about his (Man) antagonistic position toward society. The first words we hear from the Underground Man tell us that he (Man) is
``a sick man ... a wicked man ... an unattractive man'' (he)
whose self-loathing and spite has crippled and corrupted him (man). He (man) is a well-read and highly intelligent man, and he (man) believes that this fact accounts for his (man) misery. The Underground Man explains that, in modern society, all conscious and educated men should be as miserable as he (Man) is. He (Man) has become disillusioned with all philosophy. He (Man) has appreciation for the sublime, Romantic idea of
``the beautiful and lofty,'' (He)
but he (Man) is aware of its absurdity in the context of his (Man) narrow, mundane existence. The Underground Man has great contempt for nineteenth--century utilitarianism, a school of thought that attempted to use mathematical formulas and logical proofs to align man's desires with his (man) best interests. The Underground Man complains that man's primary desire is to exercise his (Man) free will, whether or not it is in his (Man) best interests. In the face of utilitarianism, man will do nasty and unproductive things simply to prove that his (man) free will is unpredictable and therefore completely free. This assertion partially explains the Underground Man's insistence that he (Man) takes pleasure in his (Man) own toothaches or liver pains: such pleasure in pain is a way of spiting the comfortable predictability of life in modern society, which accepts without question the value of going to the doctor. The Underground Man is not proud of all this useless behavior, however. He (Man) has enormous contempt for himself as a human being. He (Man) is aware that he (Man) is so overcome by inertia that he (Man) cannot even become wicked enough to be a scoundrel, or insignificant enough to be an insect, or lazy enough to be a true lazybones. The second fragment of Notes from Underground, entitled
``Apropos of the Wet Snow,'' (the Man)
describes specific events in the Underground Man's life in the 1840s, when he (Man) was twenty-four years old. In a sense, this section serves as a practical illustration of the more abstract ideas the Underground Man sets forth in the first section. This second section reveals the narrator's progression from his (Man) youthful perspective, influenced by Romanticism and ideals of
``the beautiful and lofty,'' (the narrator)
to his (Man) mature perspective in 1860, which is purely cynical about beauty, loftiness, and literariness in general.
