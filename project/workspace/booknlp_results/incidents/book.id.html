Text

Incidents in the Life of a Slave Girl opens with an introduction in which the author, Harriet Jacobs, states her (Harriet) reasons for writing an autobiography. Her (Harriet) story is painful, and she (Harriet) would rather have kept it private, but she (Harriet) feels that making it public may help the antislavery movement. A preface by abolitionist Lydia Maria Child makes a similar case for the book and states that the events it records are true. Jacobs uses the pseudonym Linda Brent to narrate her (Linda) first-person account. Born into slavery, Linda spends her (Linda) early years in a happy home with her (Linda) mother and father, who are relatively well-off slaves. When her (Linda) mother dies, six-year-old Linda is sent to live with her (Linda) mother's mistress, who treats her (Linda) well and teaches her (Linda) to read. After a few years, this mistress dies and bequeaths Linda to a relative. Her (Linda) new masters are cruel and neglectful, and Dr. Flint, the father, soon begins pressuring Linda to have a sexual relationship with him (Flint). Linda struggles against Flint's overtures for several years. He (Flint) pressures and threatens her (Linda), and she (Linda) defies and outwits him (Flint). Knowing that Flint will eventually get his (Flint) way, Linda consents to a love affair with a white neighbor, Mr. Sands, saying that she (Linda) is ashamed of this illicit relationship but finds it preferable to being raped by the loathsome Dr. Flint. With Mr. Sands, she (Linda) has two children, Benny and Ellen. Linda argues that a powerless slave girl cannot be held to the same standards of morality as a free woman. She (Linda) also has practical reasons for agreeing to the affair: she (Linda) hopes that when Flint finds out about it, he (Flint) will sell her (Linda) to Sands in disgust. Instead, the vengeful Flint sends Linda to his (Flint) plantation to be broken in as a field hand. When she (Linda) discovers that Benny and Ellen are to receive similar treatment, Linda hatches a desperate plan. Escaping to the North with two small children would be impossible. Unwilling to submit to Dr. Flint's abuse, but equally unwilling to abandon her (Linda) family, she (Linda) hides in the attic crawl space in the house of her (Linda) grandmother, Aunt Martha. She (Linda) hopes that Dr. Flint, under the false impression that she (Linda) has gone North, will sell her (Linda) children rather than risk having them disappear as well. Linda is overjoyed when Dr. Flint sells Benny and Ellen to a slave trader who is secretly representing Mr. Sands. Mr. Sands promises to free the children one day and sends them to live with Aunt Martha. But Linda's triumph comes at a high price. The longer she (Linda) stays in her (Linda) tiny garret, where she (Linda) can neither sit nor stand, the more physically debilitated she (Linda) becomes. He (Sands) brings Ellen to Washington, D.C., to look after his (Sands) newborn daughter, and Linda realizes that Mr. Sands may never free her (Linda) children. Worried that he (Sands) will eventually sell them to slave traders, she (Linda) determines that she (Linda) must somehow flee with them to the North. However, Dr. Flint continues to hunt for her (Linda), and escape remains too risky. After seven years in the attic, Linda finally escapes to the North by boat. Benny remains with Aunt Martha, and Linda is reunited with Ellen, who is now nine years old and living in Brooklyn, New York. Linda is dismayed to find that her (Linda) daughter is still held in virtual slavery by Mr. Sands's cousin, Mrs. Hobbs. She (Linda) fears that Mrs. Hobbs will take Ellen back to the South, putting her (Ellen) beyond Linda's reach forever. She (Linda) finds work as a nursemaid for a New York City family, the Bruces, who treat her (Linda) very kindly. Dr. Flint continues to pursue Linda, and she (Linda) flees to Boston. There, she (Linda) is reunited with Benny. Dr. Flint now claims that the sale of Benny and Ellen was illegitimate, and Linda is terrified that he (Flint) will re-enslave all of them. After a few years, Mrs. Bruce dies, and Linda spends some time living with her (Linda) children in Boston. She (Linda) spends a year in England caring for Mr. Bruce's daughter, and for the first time in her (Linda) life she (Linda) enjoys freedom from racial prejudice. The book closes with two testimonials to its accuracy, one from Amy Post, a white abolitionist, and the other from George W. Lowther, a black antislavery writer.
